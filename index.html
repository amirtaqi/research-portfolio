Research statement in one line Multiscale ML & Thermo-Fluid–Electrochemical Design

My long-term goal is to build the Multiscale Electrochemical Systems & Informatics Lab — a group that uses physics-based modeling, multiscale computation, and machine learning to enable both forward prediction and inverse, target-driven design of electrochemical materials, batteries, and reactors.

We work where thermo-fluids, electrochemistry, transport, and mechanics meet data: from solid-state batteries and porous carbons to electrochemical water treatment and CO2 removal, with a focus on safe fast charging, microstructure-informed materials, and MRV-ready reactors.

Research program Multiscale Electrochemical Systems & Informatics

My research connects multiscale thermo-fluid–electrochemical models with machine learning, informatics, and optimization to address forward (“what happens?”) and inverse (“what should we design?”) problems in batteries, electrochemical materials, and reactors.

Project A — Safe, Fast-Charging Energy Storage

Digital twins & multiscale ML for electro-thermal–mechanical design

  • Coupled electro-chemo-mechanical and electro-thermal models from cell to pack, resolving stress, heat, and interfacial damage.
  • Physics-informed ML surrogates and neural operators for real-time prediction, uncertainty quantification, and control.
  • Forward: map designs and duty cycles to temperature, stress, and degradation trajectories.
  • Inverse: design maps and charging/thermal protocols for safe, fast charging in EVs and grid storage.

Experience at Solid Power, Nissan, and IBM on physics-informed ML for battery degradation and Li-metal modeling.

Project B — Microstructure & Transport Informatics

From processing → microstructure → coupled transport & mechanics

  • Microstructure libraries for porous carbons, solid electrolytes, and composite electrodes (tomography + simulations).
  • Image- and graph-based structure–property models for ion/electron transport, heat conduction, and mechanical robustness.
  • Forward: predict effective properties and failure metrics from 3D microstructure.
  • Inverse: processing windows and microstructures that hit electrochemical and thermo-mechanical targets.

Builds on GAC multiscale work, nanopore hydrodynamics, and a curated ROS-materials dataset from ~500 electrodes.

Project C — Reactors & MRV for Water & Climate

Thermo-fluid–electrochemical reactors with verifiable impact

  • Advection–diffusion–reaction models of electrochemical water treatment and CO2 removal (e.g., EOIF and ocean alkalinity).
  • MRV-aware design: sensor placement, operating windows, and data assimilation for credible removal estimates.
  • Forward: predict removal, byproducts, and energy use under realistic flows and thermal conditions.
  • Inverse: design reactors and monitoring strategies that maximize removal per unit energy with quantified uncertainty.

Extends my work on gas-evolving systems, electrochemical water treatment, and EOIF demonstrations with hydrogen co-production.

Cross-Cutting Methods & 5-Year Plan

A shared multiscale modeling & ML core

  • Physics-informed ML, neural operators, and reduced-order models.
  • Uncertainty quantification, Bayesian inversion, and optimal design of experiments.
  • Open datasets, benchmarks, and reproducible code for microstructures, batteries, and reactors.
  • NSF, DOE, and ARPA-E proposals connecting Projects A–C into a unified digital-twin and informatics platform.

Teaching program Multiscale, Physics-Based, and Data-Driven Learning

My teaching connects core thermo-fluids, heat transfer, and mechanics with computation and machine learning, using backward design, active learning, and inclusive practices. Students move through a cycle: Concept → Model → Code → Experiment → Communication.

Teaching Philosophy

  • Backward design & mastery: specifications-based grading, frequent low-stakes retrieval, and clear outcomes in conceptual, mathematical, computational, and experimental skills.
  • Physics-informed data use: students derive governing equations, build simulators, and then learn ML surrogates that respect conservation laws and constraints.
  • Inclusive classrooms: structured group work, multiple modalities (board work, live coding, conceptual questions), and transparent rubrics.
  • Reproducible practice: emphasis on clean code, documentation, and version control in all projects.
